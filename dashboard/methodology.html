D8 vs D-Infinity Flow Direction
Comparing single-direction and proportional flow routing algorithms
D-Infinity (Tarboton, 1997)
$$\alpha = \arctan\!\left(\frac{\partial z / \partial y}{\partial z / \partial x}\right), \quad f_1 = \frac{\alpha_1 - \alpha}{\alpha_1 - \alpha_2}, \quad f_2 = 1 - f_1$$
Flow is partitioned between two steepest-descent neighbors proportionally to angular deviation α from facet edges.

D8 — Single Direction Limited

⚠ Limitation: Each cell routes ALL water to ONE steepest neighbor. Creates unnatural straight-line channels, especially on flat terrain (slope <0.1°).

D-Infinity — Proportional Split Preferred

✓ Advantage: Water splits proportionally between two steepest neighbors. Reduces flow concentration errors by ~40% on flat Darbhanga plains.

TWI & SCS Curve Number
Terrain wetness indexing and rainfall-runoff conversion
Topographic Wetness Index (Beven & Kirkby, 1979)
$$\text{TWI} = \ln\!\left(\frac{a}{\tan \beta}\right)$$
where a = specific upslope contributing area (m²/m) and β = local slope angle. TWI > 10 → high waterlogging risk.
SCS Curve Number Method (USDA, 1986)
$$Q = \frac{(P - I_a)^2}{P - I_a + S}, \quad S = \frac{25400}{\text{CN}} - 254, \quad I_a = 0.2\,S$$
All units in mm. CN is dimensionless (55–98). AMC III (saturated): CNIII ≈ CNII × 1.20.

TWI Heatmap — Flood Susceptibility

■ High TWI (red) = flat valleys, water accumulation zones. ■ Low TWI (blue) = steep ridges, rapid drainage.

SCS-CN Runoff Curves by Land Use

Urban (CN 90) generates ~17× more runoff than dense forest (CN 55) at 200mm rainfall. AMC III shifts all curves upward dramatically.

XGBoost Risk Model & Manning's Equation
Machine learning classification and open-channel flow velocity
XGBoost Objective (multi:softprob)
$$\hat{y}_i = \sum_{k=1}^{K} f_k(x_i), \quad \mathcal{L} = \sum_i l(y_i, \hat{y}_i) + \sum_k \Omega(f_k)$$
K = 300 trees, max_depth = 8. Output: predict_proba() for 4 risk classes (Low, Medium, High, Extreme).
Manning's Equation (SI Units)
$$V = \frac{1}{n} \cdot R_h^{2/3} \cdot S_f^{1/2}$$
V = velocity (m/s), n = roughness coeff., Rh = hydraulic radius (m), Sf = friction slope. SI coefficient = 1.0 (NOT 1.486 imperial).

Feature Importance — 300 Trees, 8 Features

Rainfall (28.6%) is dominant, but hydrological context (River Dist + Flow Acc = 39.1%) surpasses raw precipitation alone.

Manning's Velocity by Surface Type

At Rh=1.0m, S=1%: Concrete channel (n=0.012) flows at 8.3 m/s vs Dense Forest (n=0.100) at 1.0 m/s — an 8× speed difference.

Additional Key Equations
Hydrograph generation, flood routing, and dam-break analysis
SCS Dimensionless Unit Hydrograph
$$T_p = \frac{D}{2} + 0.6\,T_c, \quad Q_p = \frac{2.08\,A}{T_p}$$
Tp = time to peak (hr), A = basin area (km²), D = storm duration (hr), Tc = time of concentration. Qp in m³/s.
Flood Hydrograph Convolution
$$Q(t) = \sum_{\tau=0}^{t} P_{\text{eff}}(\tau) \cdot UH(t - \tau)\,\Delta\tau$$
Effective rainfall convolved with unit hydrograph across P50/P75/P90 IMD quantile scenarios for T+0 to T+72hr.
Muskingum-Cunge Routing
$$K = \frac{\Delta x}{c}, \quad X = \frac{1}{2}\left[1 - \frac{Q}{B\,S_0\,c\,\Delta x}\right]$$
Geometry-derived K and X (no gauge calibration). Courant condition: C = V·Δt/Δx ≤ 1 is strictly enforced with auto sub-timestep.
Ritter Dam-Break Wave
$$V_{\text{wave}} = 2\sqrt{g\,h_0}$$
Used when breach probability > 60%. h₀ = water depth behind dam (m), g = 9.81 m/s². All downstream cells → EXTREME risk.
